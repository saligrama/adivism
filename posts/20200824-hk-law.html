A Brief Look at the Hong Kong National Security Law

Posted August 24, 2020

Previous Next

Post caption

After the United Kingdom handed Hong Kong over back to China in 1997, it received an assurance that the territory could keep its independent and free economic and political system until at least 2047.

In 2020, however, that assurance seems to have come in vain. Starting with a controversial extradition bill proposed in 2019, Beijing has started to take a more aggressive stance over Hong Kong’s political future. After widespread protests, the bill was scrapped, but an even more outlandish national security law was forced through the legislature in 2020.

The new law is so loosely defined that the Chinese government could conceivably arrest any critic under the vague charge of “colluding with a foreign government.” In fact, this has already happened, with journalists and leaders of student groups in jail for violating the law. Furthermore, the law also purports its jurisdiction to be worldwide; even American citizens residing in the U.S. for decades now have warrants out for their arrest.

In this post, we explore another one of Beijing’s heavy-handed attempts to stifle criticism both within and outside its borders.
