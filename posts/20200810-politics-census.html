A Brief Look at the Politics of the Census

Posted August 10, 2020

Previous Next

Post caption

When the fledgling government of the US took its first census in 1790, its formalized count of every single resident was unprecedented. While such a count should in theory be strictly about information gathering, its operational details soon became highly polarized. Many argue that the debate over the details of the census has come to a head in 2020.

The question of who should be counted and what questions should they be asked grew to be inherently political over the coming decades and centuries. Because the results of the census determine the apportionment of seats in the House of Representatives, parties have continuously tried to use these numbers to suit their own interests. Before 1868, for example, enslaved people only counted as three-fifths of a person, a compromise between Southern slaveholders’ desire to count them for political representation but not for tax purposes.

In this post, we look at the history of the census and the long record of its attempted (and in some cases, successful) usage as a political tool. Also detailed is the Trump administration’s efforts to suppress BIPOC responses to the census, as well as the other ways the White House has tried to meddle with the census in recent weeks.
