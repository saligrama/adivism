A Brief Look at Stochastic Terrorism

Posted August 31, 2020

Previous Next

Post caption

For the past decade, our news headlines have been dominated by incidences of unrest - often racially or religiously motivated - across the country and across the world. Many such hate crimes have been characterized as stochastic terrorism, acts by “lone wolves” who are inspired by the public, high-profile demonization and dehumanization of chosen outgroups.

Indeed, the amount of hate crimes hit a 16-year high at 4,571 cases in 2018, during the middle of the Trump presidency. Under his encouragement, right-wing peddlers of hate have gained a higher profile.

In this post, we look at several incidences of stochastic terrorism in recent years, starting with the October 2018 mail bombings and continuing to just under a week ago with the Kenosha, WI shootings. With the use of racial fear as a wedge issue in the 2020 presidential election, many are concerned that cases of stochastic terrorism could continue to increase in coming months.
